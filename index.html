Project Overview

Welcome to the Salifort Motors Project Showcase! My name is Tobin Zolkowski. This project dives deep into the human side of data, exploring what makes employees thrive and what leads them to leave Salifort Motors. Think of it as a data detective story aimed at improving workplace happiness and productivity.

Using exploratory data analysis (EDA), clever feature engineering, and powerful predictive modeling, we'll uncover the key factors driving employee retention and turnover. Our journey follows the PACE framework: Plan, Analyze, Construct, and Execute, ensuring a structured and insightful approach.

The Challenge: Understanding Employee Turnover

Salifort Motors faced a common but costly challenge: higher-than-desired employee turnover. Losing employees means more than just recruitment costs; it impacts team morale, knowledge continuity, and overall productivity. The HR team needed a data-driven strategy to understand the 'why' behind departures and build a more retentive workplace.

Phase 1: Exploring the Landscape (EDA)

Our journey begins by getting to know the Salifort team through data. We examined a dataset covering 15,000 employees across 10 key attributes:

  • satisfaction_level: How happy are they? (0–1 scale)
  • last_evaluation: Performance review score (0–1 scale)
  • number_project: How many projects are they juggling?
  • average_monthly_hours: Workload intensity
  • time_spend_company: Years with Salifort
  • Work_accident: Any workplace accidents? (Yes/No)
  • left: Did they leave the company? (Yes/No - Our target!)
  • promotion_last_5years: Recent promotion? (Yes/No)
  • Department: Which team?
  • salary: Compensation level (low, medium, high)

Before analysis, we cleaned the data and prepared it for modeling. This involved handling any inconsistencies and converting categorical features like 'Department' and 'salary' into a numerical format (one-hot encoding) that our algorithms can understand.

Phase 2: Building Predictive Models

Exploratory analysis gave us valuable clues, but predictive modeling helps quantify the impact of different factors and identify the strongest drivers of turnover. We employed the PACE framework to guide this phase.

Model Performance: Which Predicts Best?

We trained and evaluated three distinct models on unseen test data to predict which employees would leave:

Logistic Regression

  • Accuracy: ~78%
  • Precision: ~73%
  • Recall: ~69%
  • F1-Score: ~71%

Interpretation: Decent baseline, but struggles to identify a large portion of leavers (lower recall).

Random Forest

  • Accuracy: 97.83%
  • Precision: 98%
  • Recall: 89%
  • F1-Score: 93%

Interpretation: Excellent performance, strong balance between precision and recall.

XGBoost

  • Accuracy: 97.96%
  • Precision: 98%
  • Recall: 90%
  • F1-Score: 94%

Interpretation: Slightly edges out Random Forest, demonstrating exceptional predictive power.

Chart 13: Model Predictive Power (ROC Curve)
Visualizes how well models distinguish between leavers and stayers. Higher AUC is better.

Chart 13 (ROC Curve) visually confirms the superior performance of the ensemble methods. Both Random Forest and XGBoost achieve an Area Under the Curve (AUC) of 0.98, indicating excellent ability to differentiate between employees who will stay and those who will leave, far surpassing a random guess (diagonal line).

Feature Importance: What Matters Most?

Understanding which factors most influence the models' predictions is key to developing targeted retention strategies. We examined the feature importance scores from our top-performing models:

  • Satisfaction Level: Consistently ranked as highly important, confirming the EDA findings.
  • Time Spent at Company (Tenure): Particularly influential, likely capturing the increased risk around the 3-5 year mark.
  • Number of Projects: A significant factor, suggesting workload balance is critical.
  • Average Monthly Hours: Work intensity clearly plays a role in turnover decisions.
  • Last Evaluation Score: Employee perception of performance and feedback matters.

Note: While both models highlight similar features, their exact ranking can differ slightly, offering complementary perspectives.

Chart 14: Key Turnover Drivers (Random Forest)
Shows the top factors predicting turnover according to the Random Forest model.

Chart 14 shows the Random Forest model prioritizes satisfaction level as the most critical predictor, followed by tenure, project count, evaluation score, and monthly hours. This strongly suggests focusing on improving employee happiness.

Chart 15: Key Turnover Drivers (XGBoost)
Shows the top factors predicting turnover according to the XGBoost model.

Chart 15 presents the XGBoost perspective. Interestingly, it ranks number of projects slightly higher than satisfaction, though both remain top factors along with tenure, monthly hours, and evaluation score. This highlights the critical interplay between workload, satisfaction, and retention.

Choosing the Champion Model

While Logistic Regression provided a baseline, the ensemble methods (Random Forest and XGBoost) offered vastly superior predictive power. Given its slight edge in performance metrics (Accuracy, Recall, F1-Score) and its robust handling of complex interactions, we selected XGBoost as our champion model for generating actionable insights and identifying employees at high risk of leaving Salifort Motors.

Phase 3: Actionable Insights & Recommendations

Our comprehensive analysis, blending EDA with the predictive power of the XGBoost model, pinpoints key areas for Salifort Motors to focus on to improve employee retention:

Key Drivers of Turnover Confirmed:

  • Low Satisfaction Levels: The most consistent and powerful predictor.
  • Critical Tenure Window (3-5 Years): A period of heightened departure risk.
  • Imbalanced Workload: Both too many (burnout) and potentially too few (disengagement) projects matter.
  • High Monthly Working Hours: Strong indicator of potential burnout.
  • Lower Performance Evaluations: May correlate with dissatisfaction or lack of growth.
  • Lack of Recent Promotions: Career progression is vital for long-term retention.
  • Low Salary: Particularly impactful for attrition in lower compensation bands.

Strategic Recommendations:

  1. Boost Satisfaction: Actively monitor satisfaction (e.g., pulse surveys). Address concerns promptly, focusing on factors identified in EDA (salary, workload, recognition).
  2. Manage Workload & Engagement: Ensure project distribution is fair. Address both potential burnout (high hours/projects) and disengagement (low hours/projects).
  3. Focus on Mid-Tenure Employees: Develop clear career progression paths and growth opportunities for employees in the 3-5 year tenure range. Offer targeted development or mentorship.
  4. Enhance Performance Management: Ensure evaluation processes are fair and constructive. Link performance to development opportunities.
  5. Recognize and Promote: Increase visibility of promotion paths and recognize contributions regularly, not just through formal promotions. Review compensation competitiveness, especially for lower salary bands.

Next Steps for Continuous Improvement:

  • Conduct qualitative analysis (e.g., exit interviews, focus groups) to understand the 'why' behind dissatisfaction and mid-tenure departures.
  • Implement targeted retention pilot programs for high-risk groups identified by the model (e.g., low satisfaction, high workload employees).
  • Regularly retrain and validate the predictive model with new data to adapt to changing workforce dynamics.
